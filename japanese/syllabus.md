# Japanese — Conversational Fluency Syllabus

**Goal:** Close the gap between functional tourist Japanese and natural, confident spoken Japanese — the kind where native speakers stop slowing down for you.

**Starting point:** Two semesters college Japanese, Duolingo, study abroad. Solid hiragana, most katakana, basic polite speech. This course skips beginner ground and goes straight for the things that actually move the needle on speaking.

**What this course is not:** A kanji course. Reading beyond hiragana/katakana is not the focus. If a kanji shows up in examples, furigana goes with it.

**Format:** Each lesson builds a grammatical or conversational concept, then you practice it live with Claude. Jump in at any lesson — you don't have to go in order if you know what you want to work on.

---

## Lesson 1 — Orientation and Level Set
*Where you are, where we're going, and a quick phonetics refresh.*

- What "intermediate plateau" means and how to get unstuck
- Pitch accent: Japanese isn't tonal, but it isn't flat either
- The mora and why Japanese rhythm sounds so different from English
- Quick pronunciation traps: ら行 (r vs. l), long vowels, っ double consonants
- Self-assessment: what to expect from your first practice conversation

---

## Lesson 2 — Plain Form and Casual Speech
*The single biggest unlock for sounding like a real person.*

- Polite form (〜ます/です) vs. plain form (dictionary form) — when each is appropriate
- How casual speech actually sounds: contractions, dropped particles, sentence-final particles
- んです/んだ — the "explanation/context" marker you hear constantly
- よ、ね、な、わ、ぞ — sentence-final particles and what each one does
- Common casual contractions: じゃない→じゃん, ている→てる, というのは→って

---

## Lesson 3 — The て-Form Family
*One conjugation that connects 30+ grammar patterns.*

- て-form construction (the rules and the irregular verbs)
- て + いる: ongoing action vs. resultant state — a distinction English doesn't have
- て + みる: "try doing"
- て + おく: "do in advance / set up for later"
- て + しまう / ちゃう: completed (often with regret or finality)
- て + あげる/もらう/くれる: the giving-and-receiving grammar that trips up everyone

---

## Lesson 4 — Conditionals
*Four ways to say "if" — each with a distinct nuance.*

- たら: general if/when, sequential events, discovery
- ば: hypothetical, logical condition ("if X were the case")
- と: automatic/inevitable result, directions and instructions
- なら: "if what you're saying is true / given that context"
- How to choose — the nuance differences that matter in conversation

---

## Lesson 5 — Expressing Opinions and Emotions
*Moving from reporting facts to having a voice.*

- と思う / と思っている — stating beliefs vs. ongoing thinking
- 〜そう: looks like / heard that (two very different uses — visual vs. hearsay)
- 〜らしい vs. 〜ようだ vs. 〜みたいだ — degrees of inference and source
- 〜気がする: "I have a feeling that..."
- Adjectives for emotions and how to use them naturally (without sounding like a textbook)

---

## Lesson 6 — Natural Conversation Flow
*The glue that makes you sound connected, not robotic.*

- Discourse markers: だから、でも、それで、じゃあ、ところで
- Filler and hesitation: あのう、えーと、そうですね
- Backchanneling: はい、うん、そうそう、なるほど — responding while someone talks
- Topic-changing and buying time gracefully
- The rhythm of Japanese conversation — why silence isn't awkward the way it is in English

---

## Lesson 7 — Reasons and Explanations
*から vs. ので vs. のに — they all look like "because" but they're not.*

- 〜から: direct causal statement, often subjective or request-based
- 〜ので: softer cause/reason, more objective, preferred for polite requests
- 〜のに: "even though / despite the fact that" — implies a contradiction or complaint
- 〜くせに: "despite / you should know better" — has an accusatory edge
- 〜おかげで vs. 〜せいで: thanks to (positive) vs. because of (negative)

---

## Lesson 8 — Common Scenarios
*The vocabulary and patterns you need for real-world situations.*

Focus areas (each drilled in practice):
- Restaurants: ordering, asking about dishes, splitting the bill
- Shopping: asking for sizes/colors, haggling politely, returning items
- Directions: giving and receiving, using landmarks
- Making plans: suggesting, accepting, declining, rescheduling
- Small talk: weather, weekend plans, complimenting something

---

## Lesson 9 — Keigo Essentials
*Just enough formal language to not embarrass yourself in business contexts.*

- The three registers: 丁寧語 (polite), 尊敬語 (respectful of others), 謙譲語 (humble for yourself)
- Most useful 尊敬語 forms: いらっしゃる、おっしゃる、なさる、ご覧になる
- Most useful 謙譲語 forms: おります、申します、参ります、いただく
- いただく vs. くれる vs. もらう — and why いただく is everywhere in business Japanese
- When keigo matters and when it doesn't — knowing the contexts

---

## Lesson 10 — Putting It Together
*Extended conversation practice with full corrective feedback.*

- No new grammar — this is all application
- Free conversation across topics: current events, opinions, storytelling
- Focus on spontaneity: forming sentences under time pressure
- Common patterns you should have automatic by now
- Personalized feedback on your specific recurring patterns

---

## How to Run Practice Sessions

Any time you want to practice speaking (in text), say something like:

- **"Let's practice"** — free conversation, I'll adapt to where you are
- **"Scenario: [situation]"** — structured role-play (restaurant, job interview, etc.)
- **"Drill: [grammar point]"** — targeted practice on one pattern
- **"Correct my Japanese"** — I'll respond in Japanese and flag errors after each exchange

During practice I'll:
- Respond in Japanese at your level, with furigana on less-common kanji
- Let you finish before flagging anything (no mid-sentence corrections)
- Note errors after each exchange with a brief explanation
- Push your grammar gently upward when you're comfortable
