# Lesson 1 — Orientation and Level Set

## The Intermediate Plateau

After study abroad, most learners can get things done. You ordered food, asked for directions, made friends. That's real. But there's a gap between "functional with effort" and "natural under pressure," and closing it feels slow because you're not failing obviously anymore — you're just not quite right.

The two main culprits at this stage:

**1. You're still using polite form as your default.** Japanese people are polite to foreigners. So your study-abroad conversations were almost entirely in 〜ます/です. That form is correct, but it's formal — it's the register you'd use with a customer or a stranger on the phone. Native speakers talking to each other use plain form, with heavy use of contractions and sentence-final particles. You've heard it, but you haven't built it in. That's Lesson 2.

**2. Your output is grammatically correct but telegraphic.** You can make true statements. What's harder is chaining thoughts naturally, hedging, qualifying, expressing that you "kind of" feel something or "heard somewhere" that something is true. That's Lessons 3–7.

This course builds those layers in order. Each one is usable on its own; together they're the difference between sounding like an advanced student and sounding like someone who actually speaks Japanese.

## Phonetics: The Parts Worth Reviewing

You already know how to read hiragana, so this is targeted at the gaps that affect speaking specifically.

### Pitch Accent

Japanese is not tonal like Mandarin — you don't need to memorize a tone for every word. But it does have **pitch accent**: each word has a pattern of high and low pitch that native speakers follow automatically.

The good news: getting pitch accent wrong rarely causes misunderstanding. The bad news: it's one of the main things that marks a non-native speaker's accent. You don't need to master it now, but being aware it exists is step one.

The basic pattern: most words start low, go high, and (depending on the word) drop back down at some point. The drop point is the accent.

| Word | Pitch pattern | Notes |
|------|--------------|-------|
| はし (bridge) | LOW-HIGH | Different from... |
| はし (chopsticks) | HIGH-LOW | ...same sounds, different pitch |
| はし (edge) | HIGH-HIGH | Three different words |

In practice, context resolves almost all ambiguity. But if you want to sound more natural, listening carefully to where native speakers drop pitch pays off more than most people expect.

### The Mora

English is **stress-timed** — stressed syllables take longer, unstressed syllables get compressed. "camera" is CA-mer-a, the first beat is long, the others are short.

Japanese is **mora-timed** — every mora gets roughly equal time. A mora is mostly just a syllable (か、き、く...), but with one important addition: the small っ (double consonant) and the ん (nasal) each count as a full mora with their own beat.

This matters for:
- **っ (geminate stop)**: きって (stamp) is ki-t-te, three beats. The stop is real time, not just a harder consonant. English speakers routinely swallow it.
- **Long vowels**: おばさん (aunt) vs. おばあさん (grandmother). The あ is literally twice as long. Getting this wrong changes the word.
- **ん**: 日本 (にほん) is ni-ho-n — three morae, three beats. The ん is not a throwaway consonant; it has weight.

### The ら行 (ra, ri, ru, re, ro)

Not R and not L. It's a flap — the same sound as the American English "t" in "butter" or "d" in "ladder." Your tongue briefly taps the roof of your mouth and comes back down. Don't roll it (Spanish r), don't round it (English r). If you say "ladder" quickly and then say ら, the contact point is the same.

This one is worth practicing because English speakers default to either R or L, and both are noticeably wrong to native speakers.

## Quick Self-Assessment

Before your first practice conversation, be honest about where you are. The goal isn't to perform — it's to calibrate.

**Things you should be able to do without thinking:**
- [ ] Conjugate a verb into 〜ます form
- [ ] Use は、が、を、に、で correctly in basic sentences
- [ ] Form a negative (〜ません、〜ない)
- [ ] Form a past tense (〜ました、〜た)
- [ ] Ask a yes/no question (〜ですか)
- [ ] Ask a content question (なに、どこ、いつ、どうして)

**Things you probably can do but might be slow:**
- て-form (the foundation of Lesson 3)
- Forming comparisons (Aより Bの方が〜)
- Expressing "can do" (〜られる、〜ことができる)

**Things that are likely fuzzy:**
- Plain form in natural context
- Choosing between たら/ば/と/なら
- The て-form family (〜ている vs. 〜てある, etc.)
- Keigo beyond the basics
- Sentence-final particles beyond ね and よ

The fuzzy things are exactly what this course targets.

## Your First Practice Exchange

To start practicing, just say **"Let's practice"** or name a scenario. A good opening for calibration:

> "Let's practice. Just have a casual conversation with me — start with something about what I did recently or what I'm interested in."

I'll respond in Japanese, let you reply, and after a few exchanges give you feedback on what was solid and what to tighten up. No pressure — the goal is to see where you actually are, not to perform.
